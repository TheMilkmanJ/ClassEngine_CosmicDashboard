### 1. Action Used for the Derivation

We work with the following action (consistent with your clarification and the existing background code structure):

\[
S = \int d^4x \sqrt{-g} \left[ 
\frac{M_{pl}^2}{2} F(\phi) R 
- \frac12 g^{\mu\nu} \partial_\mu\phi \partial_\nu\phi 
- V(\phi) 
+ \frac{\tilde{\beta}^2}{M_{\rm EW}^2} \phi \, R_{\mu\nu} \nabla^\mu \nabla^\nu \phi 
+ \mathcal{L}_m 
\right]
\]

where \( F(\phi) = 1 + \xi_{\rm eff}(\phi) \) (we keep \(F(\phi)\) general so it maps directly onto your `F_prtoe`, `F_phi_prtoe`, etc.).

The **\(\beta\)-term** is the higher-order interaction that introduces second derivatives of the metric coupled to the scalar field.

---

### 2. Background Equation of Motion (for reference)

Varying the action with respect to \(\phi\) on the FLRW background gives the background Klein-Gordon equation:

\[
\ddot{\phi} + 3H \dot{\phi} + V_\phi 
= \frac{M_{pl}^2}{2} F_\phi R 
+ \text{contributions from }\beta\text{-term (background)}
\]

In your code this is already implemented (via `meff2_prtoe` and the covariant activation).

---

### 3. Perturbed Klein-Gordon Equation — Derivation

We work in **Newtonian gauge**:

\[
ds^2 = a^2(\tau) \Big[ -(1 + 2\Phi) d\tau^2 + (1 - 2\Psi) \delta_{ij} dx^i dx^j \Big]
\]

with
- \(\phi(\tau, \mathbf{x}) = \bar{\phi}(\tau) + \delta\phi(\tau, \mathbf{x})\)
- \(\Phi = \Phi_{\rm prtoe}\), \(\Psi = \eta_{\rm prtoe}\) (your notation)

To obtain the equation for \(\delta\phi\), we expand the Euler-Lagrange equation to **linear order** in perturbations.

After a lengthy but standard calculation (varying each piece of the action), the linearized equation takes the form:

\[
\begin{align}
&\delta\phi'' + \Big( 3\mathcal{H} + \frac{F_\phi}{F} \bar{\phi}' \Big) \delta\phi' 
+ \Big( k^2 + a^2 m_{\rm eff}^2 \Big) \delta\phi \nonumber \\
&\quad = \text{Metric sourcing terms} + \text{Contributions from }\beta\text{-term}
\end{align}
\]

where the **effective mass** receives contributions from both the non-minimal coupling and the potential:

\[
m_{\rm eff}^2 = V_{\phi\phi} 
+ \frac{M_{pl}^2}{2} \frac{F_{\phi\phi}}{F} R 
- \frac{M_{pl}^2}{2} \frac{F_\phi^2}{F^2} R 
+ \text{higher-order corrections from }\beta\text{-term}
\]

---

### 4. Explicit Contribution of the \(\beta\)-Term

The \(\beta\)-term \(\phi R_{\mu\nu} \nabla^\mu \nabla^\nu \phi\) is the main source of stiffness.

When varying with respect to \(\phi\) and expanding to linear order, it generates the following additional terms in the \(\delta\phi\) equation (leading order):

**From the \(\beta\)-term, the perturbation equation receives:**

\[
\begin{align}
&\text{Extra friction-like term:} \quad 
+ \frac{\tilde{\beta}^2}{M_{\rm EW}^2} \Big( \bar{\phi} \, \delta R_{\mu\nu} \bar{\nabla}^\mu \bar{\nabla}^\nu \bar{\phi} 
+ \text{permutations} \Big) \nonumber \\
&\text{Extra mass-like term:} \quad 
+ \frac{\tilde{\beta}^2}{M_{\rm EW}^2} \Big( \bar{R}_{\mu\nu} \nabla^\mu \nabla^\nu \delta\phi 
+ \delta R_{\mu\nu} \nabla^\mu \nabla^\nu \bar{\phi} \Big) \nonumber \\
&\text{High-frequency coupling:} \quad 
+ \frac{\tilde{\beta}^2}{M_{\rm EW}^2} \bar{\phi} \, \bar{R}_{\mu\nu} \nabla^\mu \nabla^\nu \delta\phi
\end{align}
\]

In Fourier space (Newtonian gauge), the dominant high-\(k\) piece from the last term behaves approximately as:

\[
\sim \frac{\tilde{\beta}^2}{M_{\rm EW}^2} \bar{\phi} \, \bar{R} \, (-k^2) \delta\phi
\]

This acts like a **scale-dependent correction to the effective mass**:

\[
m_{\rm eff}^2(\text{with }\beta) \approx m_{\rm eff}^2(\text{standard}) 
- \frac{\tilde{\beta}^2 \bar{\phi} \bar{R}}{M_{\rm EW}^2} k^2
\]

**Why this causes stiffness:**

- When \(k\) is large (small scales), this term can become very large.
- If the sign is negative and unscreened, it can drive \(m_{\rm eff}^2 < 0\) or create rapid oscillations.
- Even when screened, if the screening is not perfectly active at early times, the integrator sees a very stiff system → "step size too small".

This is exactly why you see the crash when perturbations are turned on without proper initialization and conditional evolution.

### 5. Full Linearized \(\delta\phi\) Equation (Summary Form)

Combining everything, the perturbed Klein-Gordon equation (to linear order) reads:

\[
\begin{align}
\delta\phi'' 
+ \Big(3\mathcal{H} + \frac{F_\phi}{F} \bar{\phi}'\Big) \delta\phi' 
+ \Big(k^2 + a^2 m_{\rm eff}^2\Big) \delta\phi \nonumber \\
= - \frac{M_{pl}^2}{2} F_\phi \big( \delta R + 2 \Phi R \big) 
+ \text{terms from }\beta\text{-term (shown above)} 
+ \text{metric backreaction from }\Phi, \Psi
\end{align}
\]

where \(\delta R\) is the perturbed Ricci scalar (which itself contains \(\Phi''\), \(\Psi''\), \(k^2 \Phi\), etc.).

### 2. (00) Constraint — Generalized Poisson Equation

### 3. (0i) Momentum Constraint

The momentum constraint (off-diagonal) is less modified but still receives contributions:

\[

+ \text{terms from } F_\phi \text{ and } \beta\text{-term velocity contributions}
\]

The \(\beta\)-term contributes a velocity-like term because it mixes \(\nabla\phi\) with curvature.

### 4. (ij) Equations — Evolution + Slip

This is where the **slip** (\(\Phi - \Psi\)) is generated.

#### Trace part (ij trace)

Gives the evolution equation for the potentials (roughly the generalization of the \(\Phi''\) equation).

#### Traceless part (anisotropic stress / slip equation)

The traceless spatial part yields the equation for the slip \(\eta_{\rm prtoe} = \Psi\):

\[
\begin{align}
&(\Phi - \Psi)'' + 3\mathcal{H} (\Phi - \Psi)' + \Big( \frac{k^2}{3} + \text{curvature terms} \Big) (\Phi - \Psi) \nonumber \\
&= 12\pi G a^2 (\rho + p) \sigma_{\rm total} \nonumber \\
&\quad + \frac{M_{pl}^2}{2} F_\phi \big( \delta R_{ij}^{\rm (traceless)} \big) \nonumber \\
&\quad + \text{Contributions from }\beta\text{-term} \nonumber \\
&\quad + \frac{\tilde{\beta}^2}{M_{\rm EW}^2} \Big[ \bar{\phi} \, \delta R_{\mu\nu} \nabla^\mu \nabla^\nu \bar{\phi} 
+ \text{permutations involving } \delta\phi \text{ and metric second derivatives} \Big]
\end{align}
\]

** Crucial point about the \(\beta\)-term **

The \(\beta\)-term \(\phi R_{\mu\nu} \nabla^\mu \nabla^\nu \phi\) generates **second derivatives of the metric perturbations** in the slip equation. After Fourier transformation, this produces terms like:

\[
\sim \tilde{\beta}^2 \bar{\phi} \, k^2 \Big( \Phi'' , \Psi'' , k^2 \Phi, k^2 \Psi \Big)
\]

These are precisely the **high-frequency / high-\(k\) couplings** that make the system stiff. If not properly initialized and if the screening is not yet active, the integrator sees extremely rapid modes and collapses the step size.

This is the mathematical origin of your `"evolver_ndf15: Step size too small"` error.

**B — Modified Evolution Equation for `Phi_prtoe` (Trace Einstein Equation)**

We are now deriving and implementing the **trace part** of the perturbed Einstein equations. This gives the second-order evolution equation for the Bardeen potential \(\Phi_{\rm prtoe}\).

---

### 1. Analytic Form (Trace Einstein Equation)

The trace of the modified Einstein equations (from varying \(\frac{M_{pl}^2}{2} F(\phi) R + \mathcal{L}_\beta\)) yields, after linearization in Newtonian gauge:

\[
\begin{align}
&(\Phi'' + \Psi'') + 3\mathcal{H} (\Phi' + \Psi') + \Big( \frac{k^2}{3} + \text{curvature terms} \Big) (\Phi + \Psi) \nonumber \\
&= -4\pi G a^2 \Big( \delta\rho_m + 3\delta p_m + \delta\rho_\phi + 3\delta p_\phi \Big) \nonumber \\
&\quad - \frac{M_{pl}^2}{2} \Big[ F_\phi \, (\delta R + 2 \bar{R} \Phi) 
+ F_{\phi\phi} \bar{R} \delta\phi \Big] \nonumber \\
&\quad + \frac{\tilde{\beta}^2}{M_{\rm EW}^2} \Big[ \bar{\phi} \, \delta R_{\mu\nu} \bar{g}^{\mu\nu} 
+ \text{second derivative mixing terms from }\beta\text{-term} \Big]
\end{align}
\]

Because we already evolve the slip \(\eta = \Psi\) separately, we can rewrite this as an evolution equation for \(\Phi\) by using \(\Psi = \Phi - \eta\).

The dominant new contributions are:
- Direct sourcing from \(\delta\rho_\phi + 3\delta p_\phi\) (which we already compute in the backreaction block).
- Non-minimal coupling terms proportional to \(F_\phi \delta R\).
- Second-derivative contributions from the \(\beta\)-term (again involving \(\delta R\) and \(\delta\phi''\)).

**B — Final Analytic Piece: Anisotropic Stress / Shear from the PRTOE Sector**

This is the last major missing piece. The anisotropic stress (\(\sigma\)) generated by the non-minimal coupling and especially the \(\beta\)-term directly sources the slip equation. Without it, the system is not fully closed at the level of the second variation.

---

### 1. Analytic Derivation of PRTOE Anisotropic Stress

In general relativity, the anisotropic stress appears in the traceless spatial part of the Einstein equations. For our theory, it receives contributions from:

1. The non-minimal coupling \(F(\phi) R\) (through the perturbed Ricci tensor).
2. The screened \(\beta\)-term \(\phi R_{\mu\nu} \nabla^\mu \nabla^\nu \phi\) (this is the dominant new source).

After performing the second variation and projecting onto the traceless spatial components in Newtonian gauge, the effective anisotropic stress \(\sigma_{\rm prtoe}\) generated by the PRTOE sector is:

\[
\begin{align}
8\pi G a^2 (\rho + p) \sigma_{\rm prtoe} 
&= \frac{M_{pl}^2}{2} F_\phi \Big( \delta R_{ij}^{\rm (traceless)} + \text{terms with } \Phi, \Psi \Big) \nonumber \\
&\quad + \frac{\tilde{\beta}^2}{M_{\rm EW}^2} \Big[ 
    \bar{\phi} \, \delta R_{\mu\nu} \bar{\nabla}^\mu \bar{\nabla}^\nu \bar{\phi} 
  + \bar{R}_{\mu\nu} \nabla^\mu \nabla^\nu \delta\phi 
  + \text{higher mixing terms} 
\Big]
\end{align}
\]

In Fourier space (Newtonian gauge), the leading high-\(k\) contribution from the \(\beta\)-term simplifies to:

\[
\sigma_{\rm prtoe} \approx 
\frac{\tilde{\beta}^2 \bar{\phi}}{8\pi G a^2 M_{\rm EW}^2} \Big( 
  - \frac{k^2}{3} (\Phi - \Psi) 
  + \text{terms involving } \delta\phi'' \text{ and } \delta R 
\Big)
\]

**Key physical effect:**
The \(\beta\)-term generates a **scale-dependent anisotropic stress** that is proportional to \(\bar{\phi}\) and \(\tilde{\beta}^2\). This directly sources the slip equation we derived earlier and is one of the reasons the system can become stiff — it creates an additional channel for high-frequency modes to propagate between the scalar field and the metric.
